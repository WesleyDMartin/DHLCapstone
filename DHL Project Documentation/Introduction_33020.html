What is the DHL Cultural Learning Platform

Our vision is to create a Cultural Learning Catalogue that allows users to interact with cultural “experts” in VR environments to learn with and about cultures from around the world. With AI enabled interactivity, users can learn through their own unique and personalized conversations while simultaneously exploring VR environments that help illustrate cultural stories. The goals: Gain perspective. Build world views. Develop empathy

Leigh Cassell

Suggested by Russell Foubert and Leigh Cassell, the details of this project are still a little up in the air, but the end goal is clear: To enable learning about cultures from around the world.

Our team’s primary goal for this project is to conduct in-depth Initial Research into different platforms which could support Leighs vision for an interactive catalog. At this point, we understand our teams first goal to be to create a Prototype to perform analysis on the endpoints chosen and setup in the research phase.

Why is this Project Needed

The need for cultural understanding and empathy is, and will continue to be, at the forefront of necessary learning in our increasingly globalized and hope for a more equitable world. Conservative estimates have 10-20 million people displaced/refugees in the next 15-25 years due to the effects of climate change alone. Without cultural empathy and understanding the results of this forced migration will unfortunately be a furthering of insular xenophobic policies and increased violence. Government agencies, with the education sector at the forefront, will be tasked with creating and curating the required cultural stories that will help them enable their domestic immigration policies efficiently. Educators, most of whom are ill-equipped to act as cultural mediators, are overwhelmed by these demands and require support and resources. Meanwhile, the cultural experts are often overworked or unavailable for the prolonged, individualized experiences required for true cultural empathy/transfer to occur. For example, the requests for authentic voices from Canada’s Indigenous communities (supported by the Truth and Reconciliation Committee) is already starting to overwhelm cultural teachers/elders. Traditional models (e.g. text, audio, video) do not impact the empathetic response like interactive VR can in the same amount of time (see this study). Moreover, the ability to individualize the experience through an AI enabled (e.g. IBM Watson) interactivity allows users to deepen their learning within an authentic learning environment free from embarrassment and censure. Furthermore, the ability to continue the learning at home opens up the market to the entire population. Lastly, development of this technology has growth potential in language learning, gaming, and a variety of other educational disciplines.

Leigh Cassell
